Tuft & Needle / 2019

I’ll take my product page with mayo, please

Problem

Beautiful, custom product pages were taking too much design and engineering effort considering the frequency of launches.

Result

A new, flexible template allowed the team to launch a new product 40-80% faster while still maintaining an engaging, storytelling design.

My Role

Strategy, Leadership, Creative Direction, UX Direction, UX Design, Mentorship

In user testing, participants loved the unique, storytelling layouts of Tuft & Needle’s product detail pages (PDPs). The large photography, custom iconography, and diagrams were reported to be helpful in making a purchase decision. However, given the product launch cadence, we needed to find a way to make these pages more efficient so that design and engineering could focus their efforts on more complex problems.

We also had a consistency issue. Because we had been moving fast and iterating constantly, the Tuft & Needle PDPs were in various stages of improvements.

User testing confirms our hypotheses

User testing on the existing product pages confirmed what we knew: moving between products was confusing for customers. The pages lacked consistent patterns, making it hard to find information quickly and compare products. It also revealed how important reviews are for shoppers. The large, engaging photography, iconography, and diagrams were helpful to participants however they also revealed some issues with how the diagrams were fitting in various viewports.

Getting everyone into the room and on the same page

Both marketing and UX had a lot of thoughts about how the page should be structured. To make sure we took everything into account, we held an initial brainstorming session with marketing. We asked each participant (marketing, design, copywriters) to bring three examples of PDPs that they felt were successful and to be prepared to explain why. We then put them up on the walls to identify patterns. Talking through everyone’s selections, we made sure to capture any must-have requirements and hypotheses to test.

This initial meeting was a great way to ensure that marketing felt heard and that their requirements were factored in. There were, of course, areas where UX and marketing were in disagreement about the best course of action. We worked through each of these issues and noted any items that we wanted to A/B test.

A sandwich model emerges

As we ideated on possible structures for the PDPs, the analogy of building a sandwich started to take shape. Above all, we needed the PDPs to be a balance of flexibility and consistency. We wanted users always to be able to find important information in the same place, but we also wanted to have optional sections to add more rich content to the products that needed it. We realized that just like a sandwich, the PDPs would have components that are always required, like bread and meat. But then there would be optional add-ons — lettuce, tomato, and special sauce — depending on how fancy you wanted to get.

This analogy let us have fun with the project and helped the marketing and engineering teams get on board with the idea.

How it all comes together

Now, at the beginning of each new product launch, we have a multidiscipliary kickoff with product, design, copy, and marketing. Together we decide the high-level components of the sandwich based on the product’s features, importance and marketing strategy. Then the designer and copywriter pair to create the page.

The engineering team is finalizing the new product detail page template. Already, it allows us to launch product pages 40% faster. Once the PDP is fully integrated into our CMS, it will remove its reliance on engineering entirely. At this point, we should be able to launch product pages 80% faster than our original launch speed. This will allow design and engineering to spend more time on more complex problems.
